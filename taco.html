Problem

Procurement was running blind

Purchasing data lived in scattered, manually maintained spreadsheets. Leaders could not easily answer basic questions: where is the money going, which vendors perform, how much are we actually saving, and why do orders arrive late?

Because nothing was consolidated, problems were only noticed after the fact. Bottlenecks in the buying process, late deliveries, and vendor concentration all stayed invisible until they had already caused delays — leaving decisions to run on anecdote rather than evidence.

Objective

The question to answer

Build one self-serve dashboard that lets procurement stakeholders monitor spend, savings, vendor performance, and delivery speed — filterable by company, division, location, and category — so bottlenecks surface early and decisions rest on data, not guesswork.

Data & approach

From scattered files to a decision tool

01

Consolidate & clean

Pulled ~1,000 purchase-order records together, then cleaned and standardised them with SQL and Excel — fixing inconsistent codes, dates, and vendor names into one reliable dataset.

02

Model the metrics

Built the KPIs that matter to procurement: saving %, lead time broken down by process stage, on-time delivery rate, and spend composition by company, category, and OPEX/CAPEX.

03

Design a 5-page narrative

Structured the dashboard as a story — Executive Summary → Vendor Performance → Lead Time → Cost Saving → Transaction Detail — each page moving from headline KPIs down to drill-down detail.

04

Enable self-serve

Added consistent cross-page filters so stakeholders can slice any view by company, division, location, or category without asking an analyst.

Findings

What the data revealed

Delivery reliability

Process gap

Digging into the stage breakdown, most of that ~18-day cycle is spent in negotiation and final-price approval — pinpointing where to intervene.

From findings to action

What I'd recommend

→

Audit the urgent-order workflow. It currently adds no speed — either fix the fast-track path or stop treating urgent orders as a lever that doesn't move.

→

Target the negotiation & approval stages. They dominate the lead-time cycle, so shaving days there is the fastest route to lifting the 50.7% on-time rate.

→

Review vendor and spend concentration. With spend clustered in a few vendors and heavily OPEX, there is room to renegotiate volume discounts and reduce dependency risk.

Impact

Why it matters

The dashboard replaced scattered manual files with a single tool a procurement team can open and immediately act on — no analyst in the loop. More importantly, it converted raw transactions into evidence-ranked priorities: instead of "deliveries feel slow", the team can now see that half are late, that the urgent flag is broken, and exactly which process stage to fix first.
